Adding spinach or other leafy greens to smoothie packs is a great way to boost nutrition. Here are some tips on how to prepare ahead, measure, and decide between fresh and frozen greens.

### Ways to Add Spinach or Leafy Greens to Smoothie Packs

- **Frozen Smoothie Packs:**

**Preparation:**

- Wash and dry the greens thoroughly.

- Portion out the greens into freezer-safe bags or containers.

- Add complementary ingredients like fruits (bananas, berries), yogurt, or nut butter.

- Seal tightly and label with the date.

**Freezing:**

- Lay the bags flat in the freezer to save space and ensure even freezing.

- **Unfrozen Smoothie Packs:**

**Preparation:**

- Use fresh greens and combine them with other fresh ingredients.

- Store in airtight containers in the refrigerator for 1-2 days.

- **Tip:** Consider adding a squeeze of lemon juice to help preserve freshness.

### Measuring Frozen Chunks

- **Using a Scale:**

- If you have a kitchen scale, weigh the frozen greens in grams or ounces for accurate measurement.

- **Using Volume:**

- If measuring by volume, note that 1 cup of fresh greens typically reduces to about 1/2 cup when frozen.

- For frozen chunks, 1 cup of frozen greens is usually equivalent to about 1 cup of fresh greens.

### Fresh vs. Frozen Greens

- **Cost Comparison:**

**Fresh Greens:**

- Generally more expensive but can be bought in bulk.

- Can be frozen at home to save money.

**Frozen Greens:**

- Often cheaper and pre-washed, making them convenient.

- Retain nutrients well and have a longer shelf life.

### Tips for Freezing Fresh Greens

- **Blanching:**

- Briefly blanch greens in boiling water (1-2 minutes), then plunge into ice water to stop cooking. This helps preserve color and nutrients.

- **Freezing:**

- After blanching, drain and pat dry. Portion into bags or containers and freeze.

### Conclusion

- **Best Practice:** If you consume smoothies frequently, consider buying fresh greens in bulk, preparing them, and freezing them for convenience. This can be more cost-effective than buying pre-frozen greens, especially if you can use them before they spoil.
